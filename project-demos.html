SMS bulk-style classifier

Trained on SMS spam / not-spam labels. The score is P(bulk-style): how much the text resembles bulk or automated messaging in the dataset, not whether a message is dangerous, fraudulent, or “malicious.”

Common misconceptions · Failure case · How it works · Course report (PDF)

Common misconceptions & disclaimer

01

Bulk-style is not the same as a scam

In the training data, the spam label mostly tracks automated or mass-sent text, not whether someone is trying to defraud you. A two-factor code, a shipping update, or a flight reminder can score as bulk-style because they read like automated blasts. That does not make them untrustworthy by itself.

02

A high score is not a danger rating

P(bulk-style) reflects how much the wording matches bulk SMS patterns from the dataset, not how harmful a message is. A brand you follow might score high. A low score is not proof that a message is safe. Always use your own judgement, especially with links or payment requests.

Disclaimer

This is a coursework demo. Results are probabilistic and not for production or safety decisions.

Example failure case from small-text CNNs

Representative error

Real test: a legitimate appointment reminder containing the phrase “Reply STOP to unsubscribe” scored 95.7% spam. The model learned that opt-out language is a strong spam signal from training data, and correctly so in most cases. But legitimate services use the same phrase, causing a false positive. This is a known limitation of pattern-based models trained without sender context or opt-in history.
